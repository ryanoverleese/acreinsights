Why probes often don't show the first irrigations and rains

A quick agronomy note for late June, and why some charts show no additions after a rain or June irrigation.

Why the topsoil is so dry

The top sensor on your probe sits about 4 to 6 inches down, right in the zone that's bone dry this time of year. A handful of things are pulling moisture out of that top layer right now: evaporation off bare ground with no canopy yet, the heat, early root uptake, and overall dry conditions. Together they dry the top few inches hard and leave a real deficit in the same zone where your shallow sensor sits.

Why the water seems to disappear

Soil moisture layers are stratified: a dry top layer sitting over more moist soil deeper down, and that dry top is exactly what the top sensor is reading. When we finally catch a rain or run the pivot the first pass or two, that dry layer soaks the water up first, and very dry soil takes water in slowly and unevenly. The water is busy refilling the top before it works its way down to the sensor, so the chart can look flat even though you put water on. This is very normal, we saw the same thing in 2025, and it isn't a probe or placement problem, just the top of the profile catching up before water can move deeper.

What changes as the canopy closes

Once the crop shades the ground, evaporation off the surface drops way off, and that's the biggest drying force right now. The top stays more moist, the dry and wet layers start to blend together, water moves down through the profile a lot more readily, and your probe gets more responsive to every rain and irrigation. That's when we will start to see the percolation we're expecting.
